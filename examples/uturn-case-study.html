The Checkout That Was Costing a Fortune — Silently

A classic men's wear brand lost 39% of their Black Friday sales, and nobody knew why. I had two weeks and a Hotjar account to find out.

🖼 Hero image — UTurn redesigned checkout

The Challenge

A Brand in Its First Online Black Friday — Going Backwards

UTurn had spent years building a trusted name in classic men's wear — over 20 physical stores, loyal customers, real quality. When they launched their online store and ran their first Black Friday campaign, the expectation was a meaningful lift. What they got instead was a 39% drop in sales compared to the previous period.

The media buyer had done his part. Traffic was up. The products were there. People were finding their way to checkout. Somehow, between clicking "checkout" and completing the order, nearly everything was falling apart.

Hive Analytics brought me in as their UX and CRO lead to find the leak. The numbers told a stark story: customers had initiated checkouts worth EGP 1,700,000 — but only EGP 350,000 in actual sales completed. That's nearly five out of every six Egyptian pounds lost somewhere between intent and purchase. I had two weeks.

Research & Discovery

Thirty Session Recordings Later, the Answer Was Obvious — and Brutal

I started by connecting Hotjar to capture heatmaps, scroll maps, and session recordings. While that data accumulated, I ran a technical audit, an accessibility audit, and a heuristic UX review in parallel — I wanted to understand the full picture before watching individual users fail.

The accessibility audit surfaced a quick win: the checkout CTA was white text on a yellow background, failing both AA and AAA contrast standards. The technical audit ruled out page speed as the culprit — the site was only 0.86% slower than benchmark, not nearly enough to explain a 39% sales drop.

The heatmaps and scroll maps told a more uncomfortable story.

25% of users left before even seeing the checkout form. They landed on the page and bounced before the form came into view.
55% of users left without ever seeing the "Confirm Order" button. More than half of everyone who tried to check out never reached the point where they could complete it.
Users were clicking on non-interactive elements — and on links that navigated them away from checkout entirely, mid-flow.

Then came the session recordings. I watched about 30 of them. On mobile, I kept seeing the same pattern: someone would start filling out the form, scroll down to check their order details, keep scrolling, reach the footer — and close the tab. They never saw the "Place Order" button. They didn't abandon the checkout. They finished scrolling the page without knowing the checkout was still waiting for them.

On desktop it was a different kind of pain. The layout forced users to yo-yo left and right — order summary on the left, form on the right, back to the left to check shipping, back to the right to finish typing, then over to the left again to find the confirmation button. I watched user after user lose their place, re-read fields they'd already filled in, and give up.

The form wasn't broken. It was just invisible to anyone who didn't already know exactly where to look.

Design Process

One Wireframe, Two Hard Decisions, and a Bug We Chose to Ship

With the friction points mapped, the redesign direction was clear. I produced a wireframe quickly — this wasn't a creative problem, it was a visibility problem. The form needed to be above the fold. The CTA needed to live next to the form, not on the opposite side of the screen. The layout needed to stop pulling users' attention in two directions at once.

But two decisions required more than a wireframe to resolve.

The first was the "Cash on Delivery" radio button. UTurn's WordPress checkout used COD as a required field — removing it or repositioning it would break order tracking in the backend. Rather than fight the technical constraint, I suggested we keep the button and pre-check it by default. Users wouldn't need to interact with it at all unless they were choosing a different payment method.

The second was the harder one: moving the "Confirm Order" button turned out to require separating it from the order summary module — something the WordPress template didn't support out of the box. I sat down with the developers and walked them through session recordings of users scrolling past the button. It was the clearest argument I could make. They agreed to build the checkout page from scratch.

There was one technical hiccup with the COD pre-check: on desktop, for large basket sizes, scrolling up would uncheck the radio button due to a WordPress rendering quirk. We caught it in QA. With 94.78% of UTurn's users on mobile, we made the call to ship and fix the desktop edge case in a follow-up sprint. It wasn't perfect. But waiting for perfect would have meant another week of a broken checkout losing real money.

The Solution

Less Page, More Purchase

The redesigned checkout was a single column. Form at the top, visible without scrolling. Order summary collapsed inline rather than competing for attention in a separate column. The "Place Order" button sitting right below the last form field — not across the page, not at the bottom of an unrelated section.

We changed the CTA text color to black to fix the contrast failure. We removed links and elements that had been silently navigating users away mid-checkout. We pre-checked Cash on Delivery to eliminate a step that most users didn't need to think about.

None of it was radical. The entire change was about removing friction that had quietly accumulated — design decisions that made sense in isolation but combined into a checkout that was harder to complete than to abandon.

Reflection

The Fastest Research Is the Research You Actually Do

This project changed how I think about research timelines. The instinct in a two-week engagement is to skip the discovery and jump straight to solutions — there isn't time for proper research. But the solution here was the research. Without the session recordings, I would have been guessing. The heatmaps alone weren't enough. It was watching real users scroll past the checkout button and close the tab that gave me the conviction to ask developers to rebuild a page from scratch.

The COD decision also stuck with me. The technically clean solution would have been to remove the radio button entirely. The right solution was to understand why it existed — backend order tracking — and work within that constraint rather than against it. The best UX fix isn't always the one that looks best in a wireframe.

What I'm proudest of in this project isn't the 255%. It's that the case for change was made entirely with evidence that already existed — no expensive research, no lengthy testing cycles. The data was already there. Someone just needed to watch it.
