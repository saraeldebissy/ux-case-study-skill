# UX Case Study — Section Writing Guide

Detailed guidance for writing each section of a UX/Product case study. Read this before
drafting. Each section includes: what to include, what to avoid, handling weak data,
and example language patterns.

## 1. Hero

**Purpose**: Grab attention, establish context, signal what's interesting.

**Include:**
- Project title (evocative, not just the product name — e.g. "Redesigning Checkout for Zero Frustration" not "Checkout V2")
- Role, timeline, team size
- One sharp hook sentence: what was the core tension or the headline result

**Avoid:**
- Opening with "I was tasked with…" — passive and forgettable
- Over-explaining context before the hook lands
- Listing every tool used upfront

**Example hooks:**
- "Users were abandoning mid-flow at 68% — we had 6 weeks to find out why and fix it."
- "Three teams had tried to solve this before us. We found out why they failed."
- "A single confusing label was costing the business $200K a year."

**Confidential version:**
- "A Series B fintech startup" instead of the company name
- Keep the hook — the tension doesn't need a name

## 2. The Challenge

**Purpose**: Make the reader feel the weight of the problem before showing the solution.

**Include:**
- The concrete problem (user-facing + business-facing)
- Who was affected and how many / how often
- The stakes: what happens if this isn't fixed?
- Discovery: how did you know this was the right problem to solve?
- What constraints or context shaped your starting point

**Avoid:**
- Abstract corporate language ("enhance user experience", "drive engagement")
- Skipping the "why now" — readers need to understand what made this urgent
- Presenting the problem as already understood — show the messiness of problem definition

**Handling vague problem statements:**
If the user's input is vague, reframe around impact. "The dashboard was confusing" becomes
"Users couldn't complete their core weekly workflow without support tickets — and 40% of
new signups churned within the first 2 weeks of onboarding."

## 3. Research & Discovery

**Purpose**: Show you're evidence-driven, not assumption-driven.

**Omit this section entirely if:** the project had no formal research phase, was a very
short sprint, or was execution-focused.

**Include:**
- Methods used (user interviews, usability tests, analytics review, surveys, competitive
  analysis, heuristic evaluation, diary studies, etc.)
- Sample sizes where relevant
- 2–4 key findings that actually changed the direction
- The "aha" insight — the one thing you didn't expect

**Avoid:**
- Listing every method without explaining what you learned
- Generic findings that could apply to any project
- Presenting research as confirmation rather than discovery

**Visual opportunities:**
- Affinity map or insight clusters (generate with Visualizer)
- Quote callouts from user interviews (styled blockquote)
- Analytics funnel showing drop-off (generate chart with Visualizer)

**If research was limited:**
Be honest about constraints. "With only 5 days for discovery, we ran 4 guerrilla interviews
and a heuristic review. It wasn't exhaustive, but it surfaced three recurring patterns that
anchored our direction."

## 4. Design Process

**Purpose**: Show your thinking, not just your outputs. This is where you differentiate
yourself from someone who just executes.

**Include:**
- How many meaningful iterations (not every small tweak — the pivots)
- 1–2 key decision moments: what were the options, what did you choose, why?
- What you cut and why (constraints, user feedback, technical limits)
- How you validated direction (even informally)
- Your specific contribution if it was a team project

**Avoid:**
- Listing every deliverable produced (sketches → wireframes → prototype → handoff)
- Making it sound linear when it wasn't
- Hiding the messy bits — messiness shows real process

**Good structure for decision moments:**
> "We had two directions: [Option A] and [Option B]. Option A tested better with power
> users but confused new users. Because 80% of our traffic was new users, we went with
> a modified version of Option B and added a progressive disclosure pattern for advanced
> features."

**Visual opportunities:**
- Before/after comparison (generate with Visualizer)
- Design process flow (generate with Visualizer)
- `[INSERT: Wireframe evolution]` placeholder
- `[INSERT: Figma iteration screenshots]`

## 5. The Solution

**Purpose**: Show what you made, clearly and confidently. This should feel like the reveal.

**Include:**
- What was shipped (scope it clearly)
- The 2–3 core design decisions that define the solution
- Why each decision is the right call (user need + business rationale)
- What makes this solution smart — the insight embedded in the design

**Avoid:**
- Describing every screen or interaction exhaustively
- Hiding behind "we used best practices" without specifics
- Making it sound obvious in hindsight — show the judgment calls

**Structure suggestion:**
Lead with the solution overview, then drill into 2–3 specific decisions. Each decision
follows: *What we did → Why → What it solves.*

**Visual opportunities:**
- `[INSERT: Final design screenshots / prototype recording]` — always
- Information architecture or flow (generate with Visualizer if applicable)
- Annotated design callout (generate mockup with Visualizer for key screens if no assets)

## 6. Outcomes & Impact

**Purpose**: Prove it worked. Or if it's too early, show early signals and set up what's
being tracked.

**Include:**
- Hard metrics if available (task completion rate, conversion, time-on-task, NPS delta,
  support ticket reduction, revenue impact)
- Qualitative signals (user quotes, team feedback, stakeholder reactions)
- Timeline: when were results measured?
- Business impact (even indirectly)
- What's unresolved or still being tracked

**Avoid:**
- Vague claims ("users loved it", "it was well-received")
- Metrics without context ("up 20%" — 20% from what? Over what period?)
- Pretending everything is resolved if it isn't

**Handling no data:**
This is common. Use this approach:
1. Name what was tracked and why data isn't available yet ("The feature shipped 2 weeks ago")
2. Provide qualitative proxies: quotes, adoption patterns, support ticket changes
3. Be specific about what success looks like and when it'll be measured

**Example with no hard data:**
"We shipped 3 weeks ago — too early for statistical significance on conversion. But within
the first week, support tickets related to checkout confusion dropped by 40%, and the
customer success team flagged it unprompted in their weekly report."

**Visual opportunities:**
- Metric stat cards (generate with Visualizer)
- Before/after comparison chart (generate with Visualizer)
- Quote blockquotes (styled HTML)

## 7. Reflection

**Purpose**: Show self-awareness, growth, and professional maturity. Often the section
that makes a case study memorable.

**Include:**
- 1–2 things you'd do differently with hindsight
- What you learned (about users, the domain, yourself, the process)
- Your personal highlight or contribution you're proudest of
- What's next for this product area

**Avoid:**
- Fake humility ("I should have done even more research")
- Being too self-critical in a way that undermines the work
- Skipping this section — it's what makes you a human, not a portfolio bot

**Good reflection language:**
- "If I could go back, I'd have involved [stakeholder group] earlier — we encountered
  resistance at handoff that earlier alignment would have prevented."
- "This project taught me that the most important design decision wasn't on a screen —
  it was agreeing on success metrics before we started."

## General Tone & Voice

Match the tone reference the user provided. In the absence of one, default to:

- **First person, active voice**: "I ran", "We decided", "I pushed back on"
- **Specific over vague**: numbers, names (or anonymized labels), time periods
- **Honest about tension**: good case studies have conflict, constraint, and tradeoff
- **Confident but not braggy**: let the work and decisions speak

**Writing for recruiters and hiring managers:**
- Lead with impact — they skim, so the hero and outcomes sections carry the most weight
- Make your thinking visible — hiring managers want to see decisions, not just deliverables
- Keep process sections tight and purposeful — every sentence should earn its place
- Use plain language; avoid internal jargon or tool name-dropping without context

## Handling Weak or Missing Data

| Gap | Strategy |
|---|---|
| No metrics | Use qualitative proxies + acknowledge the gap honestly |
| Confidential project | Anonymize entity names, keep the design story intact |
| Short/rushed project | Name the constraint upfront — it becomes part of the story |
| Solo project, no research | Frame as constraint-driven design: speed + assumptions + validation |
| Unfinished / cancelled | "What I would have done next" + learnings from what was built |
| NDA prevents showing screens | Describe the solution precisely enough to be credible; use Visualizer to generate representative mockups |

## Confidentiality Patterns

When the project is confidential, use these patterns consistently throughout:

**Company**: "A Series B fintech company" / "A leading enterprise SaaS platform" / "A major
European retail chain"

**Product**: "An internal operations dashboard" / "A consumer mobile app" / "A B2B onboarding flow"

**Users**: "Operations managers" / "Small business owners" / "Enterprise procurement teams"

**Metrics**: Keep percentages and relative changes — remove absolute numbers if sensitive.
"Reduced support tickets by 40%" is safer than "Reduced support tickets from 1,200 to 720/month"

**Visuals**: For screens that can't be shown, use the Visualizer to generate a representative
mockup that conveys the design concept without exposing real UI.
