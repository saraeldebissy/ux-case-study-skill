# Narrative Patterns & Writing Benchmarks

Use these as quality benchmarks and structural inspiration — not templates to copy.
Reference them while writing to calibrate tone, sharpness, and specificity.

---

## Pattern 1 — The Tension Hook

**What it is**: Open with a specific, concrete tension. Numbers + stakes in the first sentence.

**Weak version**:
> "I was tasked with redesigning the onboarding flow to improve the user experience."

**Strong version**:
> "62% of new signups never completed their first project. We had 8 weeks and no additional
> engineering capacity to figure out why — and fix it."

**Why it works**: The number creates credibility. The constraint creates drama. The reader
wants to know what happens next.

---

## Pattern 2 — The Honest Constraint Frame

**What it is**: Name limitations upfront. It reframes weakness as professional self-awareness.

**Weak version**:
> "Due to time constraints, we weren't able to conduct extensive user research."

**Strong version**:
> "With 5 days for discovery and no budget for recruiting, we ran 4 guerrilla corridor tests
> and a heuristic review of the 3 closest competitors. It wasn't exhaustive — but it surfaced
> one recurring pattern that anchored everything that followed."

**Why it works**: Specificity replaces vagueness. The limitation becomes part of the story,
not an apology.

---

## Pattern 3 — The Decision Moment

**What it is**: Lay out a real fork in the road, explain the tradeoff, own the call.

**Weak version**:
> "We explored multiple design directions before settling on the final solution."

**Strong version**:
> "We had two directions. Option A tested better with power users — they loved the density.
> Option B was slower to learn but far less overwhelming for first-timers. Since 78% of our
> traffic was new users, we chose B — and added a progressive disclosure layer so power users
> could unlock advanced features without cluttering the default view."

**Why it works**: It shows thinking, not just doing. Hiring managers read this and think:
*this person can articulate tradeoffs under pressure.*

---

## Pattern 4 — The Thin-Data Outcome

**What it is**: When hard metrics aren't available, be specific about proxies and honest about
timing.

**Weak version**:
> "The redesign was well-received by users and stakeholders."

**Strong version**:
> "We shipped 3 weeks ago — too early for statistical confidence on conversion. But in the
> first week, support tickets tagged 'can't find X' dropped 40%, and our customer success
> lead flagged it unprompted in their team standup as 'the biggest UX win this quarter.'"

**Why it works**: Proxy signals + attribution from a third party = credibility without
fabricating metrics.

---

## Pattern 5 — The Sharp Reflection

**What it is**: End with a genuine, specific learning — not a platitude.

**Weak version**:
> "This project taught me the importance of user research and cross-functional collaboration."

**Strong version**:
> "If I could restart this project, I'd have defined 'success' with the PM before the first
> sketch — not after the first prototype. We spent two weeks going in slightly the wrong
> direction because we'd each assumed the other's definition of 'improved onboarding.'"

**Why it works**: It shows a real mistake, a real lesson, and signals that this person
grows between projects — not just within them.

---

## Hook Formula Reference

Use these formulas to draft the hero hook if the user's input is vague:

| Formula | Example |
|---|---|
| **[Metric] + [Constraint]** | "68% drop-off. 6 weeks. No new engineering." |
| **[Who] + [Pain] + [Stakes]** | "Operations managers were filing 40 support tickets a week — because one label was wrong." |
| **[Previous failure] + [Your angle]** | "Three teams had tried to solve this. We were the first to ask the users why they stopped." |
| **[Unexpected finding]** | "We thought it was a navigation problem. It turned out to be a trust problem." |
