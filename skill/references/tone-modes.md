# Tone Modes

Three named modes for case study voice and structure. Claude selects a mode based on:
1. The tone reference the user shared (URL or description) — match it
2. If no reference: the user's explicit preference (narrative / structured / hybrid)
3. If neither: default to **Hybrid**

---

## Mode 1 — Narrative

**Voice**: First-person, story-led, personal. Reads like a well-written Medium post.
**Best for**: Creative roles, agencies, product design, startups, roles where personality matters.
**Recruiter reads as**: "This person thinks in stories and can communicate design to anyone."

### Characteristics
- Flows as continuous prose, minimal headers beyond section titles
- Heavy use of "I" — owns decisions and mistakes openly
- Tension and resolution arc throughout
- Quotes and human moments included
- Reflection section is personal and specific

### Structural adjustments
- Hero opens with a scene or moment, not a meta-description
- Challenge section tells a story of discovery, not a bullet list of problems
- Process section focuses on 1–2 pivotal moments, not a comprehensive walkthrough
- Outcomes can be shorter if the human story is strong
- Transition sentences between sections — no abrupt jumps

### Example opening (Narrative mode)
> "The call came on a Tuesday. Our head of growth shared her screen: a funnel chart with
> a cliff at step 3. Sixty-two percent of people who started signup never finished it. No
> one knew why. I had six weeks to find out."

---

## Mode 2 — Structured

**Voice**: Clear, scannable, evidence-forward. Reads like a well-formatted brief.
**Best for**: Enterprise, B2B, research-heavy roles, senior IC or leadership positions.
**Recruiter reads as**: "This person is rigorous, organized, and data-literate."

### Characteristics
- Clear section headers and sub-headers
- Uses short paragraphs + occasional bulleted lists for findings/decisions
- Metrics and evidence lead; story supports
- "I" and "we" mixed — professional, not cold
- Tight sentences; no atmospheric writing

### Structural adjustments
- Hero opens with role, scope, and headline metric — no scene-setting
- Challenge section may include a brief bullet list of contributing factors
- Research section uses a methods table or structured finding list
- Process section structured as: *Problem → Options → Decision → Outcome*
- Outcomes section leads with stat cards or a metrics table

### Example opening (Structured mode)
> **Role**: Lead Product Designer — **Timeline**: Q2 2024, 8 weeks — **Team**: 1 PM, 2 engineers
>
> Signup completion had plateaued at 38% for two quarters. I led end-to-end discovery and
> redesign of the onboarding flow, resulting in a 24-point improvement in 6 weeks post-launch.

---

## Mode 3 — Hybrid (default)

**Voice**: Structured spine with narrative flesh. Scannable but human.
**Best for**: Most product design roles — balances rigor with personality.
**Recruiter reads as**: "This person can think and communicate."

### Characteristics
- Clear section headers for scannability
- Prose paragraphs (not bullet lists) for the story
- 1–2 personal moments or quotes per case study
- Metrics when available; honest proxies when not
- Reflection is personal; everything else is professional

### Structural adjustments
- Hero: hook sentence (narrative) + meta tags (structured)
- Challenge: prose story of the problem
- Research: brief methods mention + narrative finding
- Process: 1 decision moment told as a story; rest as structured prose
- Outcomes: stat cards if data exists, then 1 qualitative moment
- Reflection: personal and specific

### Example opening (Hybrid mode)
> Sixty-two percent of new signups never completed their first project.
>
> **Role**: Product Designer — **Timeline**: 8 weeks, Q2 2024 — **Company**: B2B SaaS platform
>
> We'd been watching the number for two quarters without acting on it. When it finally landed
> on my plate, engineering had one sprint to give me. Here's what happened.

---

## Applying the Mode

Once a mode is selected, hold it consistently across the entire case study:
- Writing rhythm (sentence length, paragraph density)
- Header usage (more in Structured, fewer in Narrative)
- Use of "I" vs "we"
- How findings and decisions are expressed (story vs. structured list)

If the user's tone reference doesn't map cleanly to one mode, describe the hybrid you're
going for before writing: *"Your reference leans Narrative but uses more structure than
pure Mode 1 — I'll write in Hybrid with a Narrative lean."*
